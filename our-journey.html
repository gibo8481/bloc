Our Journey

From Concept to Creation

Four students from the ATLAS Institute came together for their senior capstone to create a product that would inspire the next generation of makers. We wanted to lower the barrier to entry for fabrication by designing something joyful, simple, and empowering. As makers and engineers, we know how thrilling it is to bring an idea to life. But fabrication tools often require complex software or intimidating hardware. bloc aims to flip that narrative—removing friction so users can dive straight into creating.

Precedent Research

We began by researching existing fabrication tools and maker kits. Platforms like Tinkercad, Chomp Shop, and BitsBox inspired us with their focus on beginner accessibility and hands-on learning. However, we found that these tools were catered towards younger audiences. We saw an opportunity to create something for older beginners—middle schoolers, teens, and adults—who want to build fabrication skills without needing prior experience. Our goal was to bridge the gap between playful introduction and real-world making.

Design Criteria

From our research, we developed the following design criteria:

MUST

Be accessible: clear interface, simple instructions, and low learning curve

Be engaging: invites curiosity, encourages exploration, and keeps users excited

Promote hands-on learning: parallel learning between tactile and technological tools

SHOULD

Be portable: compact and easy to move

Be safe to use: include built-in fail-safes to ensure safe operation

Be approachable: friendly aesthetics that reduce intimidation and make the device feel fun and welcoming

COULD

Easy cleanup: built-in vacuum or dust collection to keep the workspace clean and user-friendly

Support skill growth: allow makers to import their own CAD files as they gain experience

Precision lighting: built-in light to illuminate the toolpath

Early Concept Development

Our concept development phase focused on exploring the physical form, interaction design, and visual identity of bloc. We worked to create a product that felt intuitive, playful, and cohesive from both a design and branding perspective.

Sketching

We sketched a wide variety of ideas for both the console and the enclosure. Our goal was to explore different forms, aesthetics, and user flows. The console sketches focused on housing all the necessary components while remaining intuitive to use. The enclosure sketches emphasized safety, ease of access, and how it would visually coexist with the console in a shared space.

CAD Models

We began with a primary wooden enclosure design, exploring features like a hinged lid for material access and built-in dust management. Through iterations, we refined the form into a more compact, "bloc"-like shape—emphasizing rounded, inviting edges. The console design evolved alongside it, ensuring alignment in both style and function.

Branding

From the start, we worked on branding that felt playful but not childish. Our visual language was designed to complement the product and instruction booklet—creating a cohesive, recognizable experience that welcomes users without overwhelming them.

Prototyping & Iteration

We began by assembling the machine and testing it together as a team—getting a feel for how users might interact with the system. With a rough version of our Innovation Guide, we invited users to try the device and provide feedback on clarity, ease of use, and overall experience.

Human Experience Testing

To refine the console interface, we created a rough physical prototype that allowed users to place buttons and controls where they felt most natural. This helped us understand user expectations and ergonomics early in the process.

From the human experience testing, we gained insights into how users naturally interact with the interface. These placements informed our design iterations, which focused on layout, ergonomics, and ease of use. As the console evolved, we aligned its form and visual style with the enclosure—creating a cohesive and inviting interaction experience.

Enclosure Prototyping

Our first enclosure was a quick prototype made from 3D-printed parts and MDF. From this version, we learned that the drawer was unnecessary and that a shop-vac-style hose system would be more effective for dust collection.

The second prototype featured more refined 3D-printed parts designed for easy assembly using screws. We replaced the drawer with a wooden base and backing, which added stability and simplified the design. This version served as a functional test unit—allowing us to run the machine and console together and showcase the system in action.

Iteration through Feedback

We continued refining the enclosure and console side-by-side—rebuilding, testing, and tweaking as we went. With each version, we also tested the updated Innovation Guide to ensure the experience remained accessible, intuitive, and fun.

Final Product

The final bloc system is compact, joyful, and beginner-ready. Users operate the CNC with arcade-style joysticks and colorful buttons, navigating a simplified control scheme. The enclosure is made from sustainable materials—laser-cut wood and recycled PETG—and the instruction booklet walks users through their first project with illustrated, step-by-step guidance.
